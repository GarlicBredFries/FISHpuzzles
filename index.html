Starting Questions

How does puzzle hunting work?

Puzzle hunts are generally different from puzzles you may be familiar with. They may provide you with some infromation, but there is often an expectation that you will not know everything about the puzzle. The goal is to be able to hunt for the information and put it together to solve the puzzle. Using the internet is often required or at least highly suggested.
In contrast, jigsaw puzzles and logic puzzles (like sudoku) are generally able to be solved by just solving the puzzle on its own.

I have no idea where to start. What do I do?

You are often given more than the puzzle content itself. Look at the title and flavor text to see if you can get a grasp on the puzzle. You are not expected to, nor should you expect to, be able to solve the puzzle on first glance. Try some things before quitting the puzzle.
It is also necessary to take breaks. Fatigue is an important issue when it comes to puzzle hunting. Brains get tired, and it can be hard to tell sometimes.

I solved what I think is the puzzle, but I still don't have an "answer". What do I do?

There are usually two parts when solving a hunt puzzle: the puzzle itself and the extraction. It is likely that you are stuck on the extraction. The extraction can be discouraging because it can act like a second puzzle. There are many common tactics for extraction, such as the A1Z26 cipher or matching letters.
The constructor will usually try to make the extraction fair, but it can be really difficult to gauge the difficulty. If you are interested in creating your own puzzle, test solvers will be able to provide feedback on the difficulty.
